Panic Attack vs Anxiety Attack: Key Differences Explained

The terms "panic attack" and "anxiety attack" are often used interchangeably — but they describe distinct experiences with important differences. Understanding these differences can help you or someone you care about respond more effectively and seek the right support.

Overview of the Differences

The key distinctions are in onset, intensity, duration, and relationship to triggers. Panic attacks tend to come on suddenly and intensely; anxiety attacks build more gradually and are usually tied to a specific stressor.

Note:

"Anxiety attack" is not an official clinical diagnosis — it is a colloquial term. "Panic attack" is a recognized clinical term defined in the DSM-5 (Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders). If you are experiencing either, consult a healthcare professional.

What is a Panic Attack?

A panic attack is a sudden surge of intense fear or discomfort that peaks within minutes. It is one of the most frightening experiences related to mental health — many people experiencing their first panic attack believe they are having a heart attack or dying.

Symptoms of a Panic Attack

  • Sudden, intense fear or sense of impending doom
  • Racing or pounding heartbeat (palpitations)
  • Shortness of breath or feeling smothered
  • Chest pain or tightness
  • Dizziness, lightheadedness, or feeling faint
  • Tingling or numbness (especially in hands and face)
  • Sweating, trembling, or shaking
  • Nausea or stomach distress
  • Feeling detached from yourself (depersonalization) or your surroundings (derealization)
  • Fear of losing control or "going crazy"

Panic attacks typically peak within 10 minutes and resolve within 20–30 minutes. They can occur with or without an identifiable trigger (unexpected panic attacks are a hallmark of Panic Disorder).

What is an Anxiety Attack?

An "anxiety attack" refers to a period of heightened anxiety, often building gradually in response to a specific stressor. It is less abrupt and intense than a panic attack, but can be prolonged and distressing.

Symptoms of an Anxiety Attack

  • Gradual building of worry or dread
  • Muscle tension
  • Restlessness or irritability
  • Increased heart rate (less dramatic than panic)
  • Difficulty concentrating
  • Fatigue
  • Sleep problems

Unlike panic attacks, anxiety attacks are usually tied to a specific stressor (an upcoming exam, a medical appointment, a confrontation) and tend to be more prolonged.

What to Do During an Attack

During a Panic Attack

  • Remind yourself it will pass: Panic attacks, though terrifying, are not dangerous and typically peak within 10 minutes
  • Controlled breathing: Inhale slowly for 4 counts, hold for 4, exhale for 4 (box breathing)
  • Grounding technique (5-4-3-2-1): Name 5 things you can see, 4 you can touch, 3 you can hear, 2 you can smell, 1 you can taste
  • Stay where you are: Fleeing the situation can reinforce panic

During an Anxiety Attack

  • Address the stressor: If possible, take action on the specific worry triggering the anxiety
  • Deep breathing and progressive muscle relaxation
  • Talk to someone: Verbalizing worries reduces their intensity
  • Mindfulness: Bring attention to the present moment rather than future worries

Treatment Options

Panic Disorder is typically treated with CBT (especially exposure therapy) and/or medication (SSRIs, SNRIs). See: CBT Techniques for Anxiety.

Anxiety responds well to CBT, mindfulness-based therapies, and lifestyle changes. If anxiety is significantly impacting your life, consult a professional. See: When to See a Therapist.
